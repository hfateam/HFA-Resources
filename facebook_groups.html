Getting students & referrals

Using Facebook Groups

Facebook groups are still a real source of students and referrals — you just have to use them differently than you would have in 2020. This page walks you through what actually works right now, what Ciara handles on our end, and how you can jump in.

Why this matters

Some charter schools require that a currently enrolled student requests you as a vendor before you can apply — which means you need to connect with families directly before you even start the application. Facebook groups, where homeschool parents ask each other for recommendations every single day, are one of the most practical places to make that happen.

Even once you're already approved at several schools, staying present in these groups means you're there when a parent posts: "Does anyone know a good Spanish tutor for my 4th grader?" That's your moment. The goal isn't just visibility — it's being the person who actually shows up when someone's looking.

Three strategies — ranked by what works now
Jump into conversations organically Best ROI
No risk of Facebook jail. Builds real relationships. Actually converts.

Scroll the groups you've joined and look for posts where parents are asking for recommendations — "in search of art classes," "anyone know a good creative writing program," that kind of thing. If what you offer genuinely fits, reply.

The key is to not lead with an ad. Ask a clarifying question first if you can — "Are you looking for online or in-person?" — and share your info naturally once there's a real exchange. Parents respond to a conversation. They scroll past a flyer.

Also worth keeping an eye on: curriculum recommendation threads, calls for beta testers, and posts where parents are sharing what their kids are into. Those are soft openings that don't require you to pitch anything.

Worth trying: If a parent posts asking what their kid loves to draw (or read, or build), answer genuinely — then mention that you actually teach that. It's the difference between being helpful and being an ad, and parents notice.
Post a referral request Recommended
For schools that require a student to request you first.

Some schools won't let you become a vendor until a currently enrolled student requests you. This post is how you get that request. Be direct about what you need, and make it genuinely worth their time — offer something valuable to the first person who helps you out.

You don't have to give away a full class. A bonus session, a free resource, or a discount works just as well. The point is to give them a real reason to say yes.

Template — adapt this for your business
Are you enrolled at [School Name]? I'm applying to become a vendor and I need one student to request me to get started. In exchange for the referral, I'd love to offer [your offer — e.g., a free session / first month at a discount / a free downloadable resource]. You don't need to complete anything before referring me — the process is quick and easy. A little about me: [2–3 sentences about your experience, what you teach, and what makes your classes worth it]. Only the first person to reach out gets this — feel free to comment below or DM me!
Watch out for Facebook jail: Don't copy-paste the same message into multiple groups back to back. Facebook flags repetitive posting and can lock you out of your account. Space out your posts, reword each one, and join groups gradually — one or two a day max.
Post a flyer or promotional ad Optional
Lower ROI than it used to be — but still an option in groups that allow it.

Posting a flyer-style ad used to work really well — like, 25 comments in 20 minutes well. That's not the reality anymore. Most groups have tightened their rules, and even in the ones that still allow promotional posts, they tend to get ignored now.

That said, some groups actively welcome vendor posts, and some parents do find their vendors this way. If you want to try it: check each group's rules first (usually pinned or in the About section), follow their posting frequency limits exactly, and reword every post — don't copy-paste between groups.

Where it still gets traction: if you have a freebie, a free trial, or you're looking for beta testers. That framing gets real responses. A straightforward ad usually doesn't.

Quick reminders
Join gradually
One or two groups per day. Joining too many at once triggers Facebook's spam filters.
Check the rules
Groups update their rules often. Read the pinned posts and About section before you post anything.
Make your profile private
Keeps competitors from seeing which groups you're active in. Find it under your profile settings.
Vary your wording
Reword every post. Repetitive copy across groups gets flagged — even if the message is similar.
