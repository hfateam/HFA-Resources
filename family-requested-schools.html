01 — Start Here
→ Why these schools work differently (and why that's good news) +

For most schools, you go find the application and fill it out. For this group, you can't — and that's actually fine, because the school does most of the work for you.

These schools only add a vendor when a family asks for one. So the request starts with a parent, goes to the school's vendor coordinator, and the coordinator comes to you. Your part is small: get a family to make the ask, then be ready when the coordinator reaches out.

⚠ Every client offers something different, so treat this as a starting point, not a script to copy line for line. Use your own judgment about what fits your niche, and anywhere you see something like this in the templates below, it's a placeholder — swap in your own details before you send anything. Questions? Reach out anytime.
02 — The Flow
→ How the request actually works, start to finish +

Here's the whole sequence so you know what to expect:

1. The family asks
A parent at the school contacts their vendor coordinator and says they'd like to work with you.
2. The coordinator reaches out
The school's coordinator contacts your business, letting you know a family wants to use you.
3. You get started
From there you begin the vendor process — but the coordinator leads it and tells you exactly what they need.

So unlike the email or application schools, you're not chasing anything down. The catch is the opposite problem: nothing happens until a family kicks it off. Which is why the rest of this guide is about gently making that happen.

03 — Be Ready
→ What the coordinator will ask for +

When the coordinator contacts you, they'll most likely ask for the same core documents every other school wants — which is exactly why building your standard packet first pays off here. If it's ready, you can respond the same day instead of scrambling.

Have these on hand before any of this starts:

— W-9
— Certificate of Insurance
— Proof of business legitimacy
— Background check / COR status sorted
— Your services description

(That's your standard packet — the first lesson on this page. If you haven't built it yet, start there.)
⚠ One thing worth knowing: each of these schools is its own separate approval. Some of them belong to the same alliance — Sequoia Grove, Central Valley, South Schools — but being approved at one does not automatically get you into its sister schools. Each one needs its own family to start the request.
04 — Make the First Move
→ Tell your families (email / newsletter) +

If you've worked with families before, or you have an email list, this is your single highest-leverage move. A lot of parents have no idea they can request a specific vendor — so just tell them, and tell them how.

Email / Newsletter Template

Subject: Want to take my classes through your charter school?

Hi [everyone],

Quick note for any of you using a charter school for homeschooling — I'd love for you to be able to put your funds toward my [WHAT YOU TEACH — e.g. online art classes].

For a handful of schools, this actually starts with you, the family. If your school is one of these — [LIST A HANDFUL OF YOUR SCHOOLS] — here's all you do:

  1. Check your school's vendor directory for [YOUR BUSINESS NAME].
  2. If you see me listed, you're all set — go ahead and request my classes.
  3. If you don't see me listed, just reply to this email and I'll check on it for you.

That's it — if I'm not in your school's directory yet and it's one where a family needs to get things started, I'll take it from there.

Thank you! Can't wait to [create / draw / make art] with your kids.

[YOUR NAME]
[YOUR BUSINESS NAME]
[YOUR WEBSITE]

⚠ Listing the specific schools that apply to you is more effective than a general ask — a parent who sees their own school named is far more likely to act. If you're not sure which of your families are at these schools, the "reply and I'll check" version still works great.
05 — Make the First Move
→ Watch the Facebook groups +

This is the lowest-effort way to find families who are already looking. Homeschool and charter Facebook groups are full of parents asking for class recommendations — so rather than posting into the void every day, just watch and respond. When someone asks for a class you teach, that's a warm lead handing itself to you.

A light daily habit works better than a big push: skim the groups, answer the people already asking, and post your own ask now and then to stir up new ones.

Replying to someone already asking

Hi [NAME]! I teach [WHAT YOU TEACH] and would love to work with your family. Depending on your charter school, you may just need to ask your vendor coordinator to add [YOUR BUSINESS NAME] — happy to walk you through it. Feel free to message me!

Your own post / ask

Hi everyone! If you're using charter school funds and looking for [WHAT YOU TEACH — e.g. online art] classes, I'd love to work with you. For some schools you just let your vendor coordinator know you'd like to add [YOUR BUSINESS NAME], and they'll reach out to me. Comment or message me and I'll help you figure out the steps for your school!

⚠ Check each group's rules before promoting — some have specific days or threads for vendor posts. Responding to existing requests is almost always welcome; unsolicited promo sometimes isn't.
