Because you're outside of California, you need a location that will roll your fingerprints onto physical FD-258 hard cards — ink on paper. California's Live Scan system is only available to California residents, and other states' digital fingerprinting systems are not compatible with it. Physical hard cards are the only option for out-of-state applicants.

How to search: Google "FD-258 fingerprint cards near me" or "ink fingerprint cards [your city]." You can also try "hard card fingerprinting California DOJ [your city]."

Good places to look: local police department, sheriff's office, UPS Store locations (many offer this), and private fingerprinting companies.

Before you book, check the location's website for two things:

They offer FD-258 ink hard card fingerprinting for out-of-state California DOJ submission
You can leave with your completed cards the same day — same-day is strongly preferred (see below)
⚠ Same-day cards vs. mailed cards — this matters. Some locations roll your prints and then mail the completed cards to you a few days later. Others hand them to you right there at the appointment. Choose same-day whenever possible — it keeps your timeline on track and removes a variable you don't need.
✓ What you want
FD-258 ink hard cards, completed and handed to you same day at the appointment
✓ Also works
A location that mails completed cards to you — factor in 2–3 extra days before you can mail to CA DOJ
✗ Not what you need
Digital fingerprinting only — this will not work for out-of-state CA DOJ submissions. You must have physical FD-258 hard cards.

Once you have both completed cards in hand, you're ready to mail to CA DOJ.

02 — After You Mail
→ I mailed my fingerprint cards to CA DOJ. What happens now? +

Now you wait. CA DOJ processes hard card submissions manually, so it takes longer than a digital submission — but it's a normal, well-established process and you don't need to do anything while it's in motion.

Expected timeline: CA DOJ typically processes hard cards within 2–4 weeks of receipt. Allow a little extra buffer on top of that for mail transit time getting to Sacramento.

Two things to do right after mailing:

1
Save your tracking number and send it to your HFA program contact so we can confirm DOJ received your envelope.
2
Keep your preliminary approval letter from CA DOJ. That letter has your Agency REF number on it — you'll need it for reference if any questions come up.
⚠ There is no online status tracker for out-of-state hard card submissions. Your tracking number confirming DOJ received the envelope is the best confirmation you'll have while you wait.
03 — If You Haven't Heard Anything
→ It's been a few weeks and I haven't heard anything. Is that normal? +

If it's been fewer than 4 weeks since DOJ confirmed receipt of your cards — yes, totally normal. No news is not bad news here.

If it's been more than 4–6 weeks and you still haven't heard anything:

04 — Once You're Approved
→ What does COR approval mean — and what's my ORI/COI number? +

Your ORI/COI number — your unique agency identifier — is already on the preliminary approval letter CA DOJ sent at the start of this process. That's the letter that triggered your fingerprinting. Hold onto it.

When charter schools and ESA programs ask whether you've been background checked, or ask for your COI number — your answer comes from that letter. That's the document that proves your COR status.

Practically, here's what COR approval means for you day-to-day:

Your HFA program team handles background check submissions on your behalf — you don't need to run them yourself
When charter schools or ESA coordinators ask for your COI/ORI number — reference your preliminary approval letter
You are the named Custodian of Records for your agency — meaning CA DOJ recognizes you as the designated contact for all related matters
COR approval does not expire — once confirmed, you're confirmed
⚠ If your name, address, phone, or email ever changes — you are required to notify CA DOJ. See Card 05 for the full list of your ongoing COR responsibilities.
05 — Your COR Responsibilities
→ What are my ongoing responsibilities as Custodian of Records? +

As COR, you agreed to a set of responsibilities with CA DOJ when you were confirmed. Here's the official list in plain language:

Keep records confidential
Criminal record information (CORI) from DOJ is confidential. You cannot share it with anyone not authorized by law. Violations are a misdemeanor under Penal Code section 11142.
Secure storage
Any CORI your agency holds must be kept in a secured, locked area — separate from regular personnel files.
Use only as intended
Criminal record information can only be used for the purpose it was requested. It cannot be repurposed for anything else.
Written access policy
Your agency should have a written policy covering who can access CORI, how it's stored, and how it's destroyed when no longer needed.
Staff training
Anyone with access to CORI must be trained in its secure handling, storage, and destruction.
Notify DOJ of changes
If your agency name, address, phone, or COR contact information changes — you must notify CA DOJ. Don't let your contact info go stale.
DOJ audits
DOJ may audit agencies using CORI to confirm compliance. This is rare but worth knowing about.
⚠ These are CA DOJ's official COR duties. If you have questions about what any of these require in practice, reach out to your HFA program contact or CA DOJ directly at COR@doj.ca.gov.
