01 — Why This Matters
→ Why do Google reviews matter for an online teacher? +

Charter schools and ESA coordinators Google you. Parents Google you. When they do, a Google Business Profile with real reviews is one of the fastest signals that you're a real, established provider — not someone who just filled out a form.

Reviews on Google live outside any single platform. Your Outschool reviews only show up on Outschool. Your Google reviews show up everywhere — in search results, on Maps, any time someone types your business name. They follow you no matter where you're teaching.

This matters especially as you expand into charter school and ESA funding. The coordinators approving your services aren't browsing Outschool — they're Googling your business name. Give them something to find.

What a good Google profile looks like:

— Your correct business name and a working website link
— At least 5 reviews, all from real students or families
— A profile photo (your logo or a photo of your work — not a stock image)
— Responses from you on at least some of the reviews

That's it. You don't need 100 reviews. Even 5–10 solid ones make a real difference.
02 — Getting Set Up
→ How do I set up a Google Business Profile if I don't have one yet? +

Go to business.google.com and sign in with the Google account you use for your business. If you already have a profile listed there, you'll claim it. If not, you'll create one from scratch.

1
Go to business.google.com and click "Manage now"
2
Enter your business name — use the exact name you use everywhere else (on your website, your directory listings, etc.)
3
Choose your business category — "Tutoring service," "Art school," or "Educational institution" are common fits for online teachers
4
When asked for a location, choose "I serve customers at their location" — this lets you list your service area (like "United States") without giving out a home address
5
Complete Google's verification steps — they'll walk you through it. This part can take a few days, so do it now
⚠ You need to be verified before reviews will show up publicly on your profile. Don't skip this step — it's what makes everything else work.
⚠ Google may ask for a mailing address. You don't have to show it publicly — just enter it to satisfy the verification requirement, then choose to hide your address from your public profile once you're set up.

03 — Your Review Link
→ How do I get a link to send people so they can leave a review? +

Once your profile is verified, Google gives you a short direct link that takes people straight to the review box — no searching required. This is the link you'll share everywhere.

1
Log into business.google.com and go to your profile dashboard
2
Look for "Get more reviews" or "Share review form" — it's usually right on the Home tab of your dashboard
3
Copy the link — it will look something like g.page/yourbusinessname/review
4
Save this link somewhere easy to find — in a notes doc, your email drafts, whatever works — because you'll use it over and over
Put this link in your email signature, your welcome email to new students, and any post-class follow-up. The easier you make it for someone to click and leave a review, the more likely they actually will.
04 — Who to Ask
→ Who can I ask for a Google review — and who shouldn't I ask? +

Anyone who works with you outside of Outschool is fair game. Anyone you're connected to only through Outschool is more complicated — the next card covers that in detail.

✓ Great to ask
Current or past students from your own website, email list, or independent classes — anyone you have a direct relationship with outside of Outschool
✓ Great to ask
Charter school or ESA families who enrolled with you through a funding program (not through Outschool)
✓ Great to ask
Colleagues, collaborators, or community members who genuinely know your work and can speak to it honestly
⚠ See next card first
Outschool students — read Card 05 before reaching out to anyone you met through that platform
✗ Don't ask
Family members or close friends who haven't actually taken your classes — Google can detect and remove reviews that look fake
05 — The Outschool Situation
→ Can I ask my Outschool students to leave a Google review? +

This is where you need to be careful. Outschool's policies require all communication with parents to stay on their platform. They explicitly prohibit using Outschool contacts to promote outside services or do outside business.

⚠ Sending Outschool families a message through Outschool asking for Google reviews is a policy violation risk. It is not worth risking your account over.

That said, here's what can work:

If a family already follows you off-platform — they're on your email list, follow your social media, or signed up through your own website independently — they're not exclusively an Outschool contact anymore. You can reach them through those other channels.

If a family has moved to working with you directly outside of Outschool, they're a direct client now. Totally fine to ask them.

The goal going forward: as you grow your independent presence, get families onto your own email list before you ask for reviews. That way you're never relying on Outschool's messaging system to do it.
Outside Outschool = fair game Outschool messaging = policy risk
06 — How to Ask
→ What's the best way to actually ask someone for a review? +

Ask right after a good moment — end of a class, when a student shares something they made, when a parent says something kind. Timing matters more than the perfect wording.

Keep it short and personal. Something like this works great:

"Hey [name] — it would mean so much if you'd be willing to leave me a quick Google review. Honest feedback helps other families find me, and it only takes a minute. Here's the direct link: [your link]"

That's really it. Don't overthink it. A short personal message almost always works better than a fancy template.

A few places and formats that work well:

Post-class email — send a follow-up after the last session of a course with your review link at the bottom
Email signature — a simple line like "Enjoying your classes? Leave a review here →" with your link
Welcome email to new students — mention upfront that you'd love a review once they've experienced your classes
A direct personal message — for students you know well, a quick personal ask almost always outperforms anything automated
⚠ Never offer discounts, free classes, or gifts in exchange for a review. This violates Google's policies and can get your reviews removed — or your profile flagged.
07 — Responding to Reviews
→ Do I need to respond to reviews once I have them? +

Yes — and it matters more than most people realize. When a charter coordinator or a new parent looks at your profile, they're not just reading the reviews. They're watching how you respond to them.

For a positive review: Keep your response warm and short. Thank them by name if you can, reference something specific from what they wrote, and that's enough. You don't need a paragraph.

For a negative review: Don't ignore it, don't get defensive, and don't argue publicly. Acknowledge it calmly, offer to connect directly to work it out, and move on. One critical review handled gracefully often looks better than a profile with zero negative reviews at all — it shows you're real and you care.

A profile where the owner responds to reviews looks active and trustworthy. A profile with 20 reviews and zero responses from the owner looks abandoned — even if all the reviews are glowing.

Your Action Checklist
1
Go to business.google.com — claim your existing profile or create a new one
2
Complete Google's verification process — reviews won't show publicly until this is done (start here first, it takes the longest)
3
Get your direct review link from your dashboard and save it somewhere easy to find
4
Make a list of 5–10 people outside of Outschool who know your work and could leave an honest review
5
Send each of them a short personal message with your review link — keep it simple and genuine
6
Add your review link to your email signature and any post-class follow-up emails going forward
7
When reviews come in, respond to them — positive and negative — so your profile looks active
